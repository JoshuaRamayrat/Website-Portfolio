Hybrid Quadplane

This was our Senior Design where we developed a prototype of a hybrid "quadplane" with the intent of developing a system to better suit the needs of search-and-rescue teams. The problem with most drones is their use of multirotors to search for missing hikers over long-distance routes. A quadplane uses the efficiency of a distance glider with the utility of a drone.

I helped with brainstorming the original idea through motivating team members, designing the avionics and body hardware in Solidworks, managing our Bill of Materials, and assisting with troubleshooting the graphical user interface to actuate the avionics.

As suggested by the videos, we developed it to the extent of testing the motor configuration and running the geometry of the plane through computer simulations in Abaqus and Star-CCM. With more time, we would have made further mechanical design adjustments and flight tuning.

The firmware written for the Pixhawk flight controller was open source and available on Github. Most of the software was object-oriented C++.

  • Designed by: Joshua Ramayrat
  • Date: Fall 2017 - Winter 2017
  • Associates: Nick Gagliardi, Kiavash Moazzami, Francesca Caruso, Nicholas Keyes

The Lucifer Effect

This book is a reflection on the activities taking place within a simulated prison run by a research psychologist. Its not really an actual government-decreed prison but rather a prison of the mind. People take on roles as either a prisoner or guard and they act accordingly whether deliberate or by force. The entire experiment and its correspondents are deeply reflective and analogous to the everyday life of society. We would like to believe ourselves as entirely self-interested and uninfluenced individuals but we are constantly bombarded by the external environment, situational factors, and figures of authority.

These authority figures exist in individuals, groups, and societies. We are constantly influenced by it and the decisions we make in the present are reflective of our hopes for the future and the results of our past. I stopped doing activity X because it gave me Y results with this certain crowd of people. I stopped behaving in an X way because person Y didn't like it. When we're not trying to get positive results we may rebel.

We rebel just as the prisoners do in Dr. Zimbardos lab both physically and psychologically. Either that or we act obediently. The obedient prisoners were quiet and agreed to everything, regardless of the objectively ridiculous behavior of the guards. As a result they were awarded with less severe punishments and parole. We behave in the same way to avoid negative feelings of torment or social ostracism. However, we may often rebel in the belief that our vision is right and not theirs. What we think of as a normal reaction to our surroundings, others can interpret that as a rebellious or evil action. You can apply this concept to small and large scale systems.

Dr. Zimbardo also noticed the dual personality split of the prisoners. In the depths of a prisoner's mind, he's aware of his true role in society but often blurs such a distinction as suggested by his personal diary. That leads into another question - to what extent do existing systems define who we are?

Flow: The Pyschology of Optimal Experience

Review

A person is happiest when they are in deep work. They forget about time, their location, or even the thought of their own self because of their complete focus on the moment. Flow is when were happiest and every person from every walk of life would do anything to be in this state of mind. Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience, written by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi, describes conditions and mindsets that a person must be aware of before they can optimally experience their life. It also explains what can thwart us from achieving such an experience.

Leadership BS

When it comes to leadership, people generally have a rosy colored view of a nice boss, a colleague who wants the best for you, and the subordinates who wish to serve you. This is never really the case and those who want to face reality must handle very harsh truths. You must be narcissistic; look out for your own interests; pad certain truths and tell soft lies; win the hearts of superiors; and fabricate identities to fit the time and place that you're in - all of this to truly contribute to your organization.

A person never has a consistent identity as they can take on so many different roles within society - whether you're a student, professor, engineer, son, daughter, husband, motorcyclist, gang member, or drug addict, you will begin to take on personalities and opinions that result from the situation you've placed yourself in. It is flawed to have a baseline personality to project across all walks of life especially in an organizational setting.

It's also faulty to to completely model yourself after revered authority figures in society like Gandhi, Buddha, Rosa Parks, or Bill Gates when they give you very uplifting depictions of what it means to be a good leader. Their comforting advice accounts for a fraction of a percentage of what actually makes good leaders. Most authority figures have their own faults whether its in their lifestyles, actions, or personalities. Moreover, such faults are magnified and made a bigger deal than they actually are. Most leaders are regular people in reality - they adopt qualities that make them fit to manage or control certain situations. This tendency to dramatize their negative actions results from placing them on high pedestals. They're envisioned as almost perfect but if one fault is exposed, it can undermine all positive opinions.
