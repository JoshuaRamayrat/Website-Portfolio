Hybrid Quadplane

This was our Senior Design where we developed a prototype of a hybrid "quadplane" with the intent of developing a system to better suit the needs of search-and-rescue teams. The problem with most drones is their use of multirotors to search for missing hikers over long-distance routes. A quadplane uses the efficiency of a distance glider with the utility of a drone.

Although I wasn't officially the team captain, I played fairly huge roles in the conception of this product such as brainstorming the original idea, designing the entire avionics and hardware in Solidworks, selecting and purchasing all of the electronic components, and troubleshooting the pre-built software to enable the plane to start.

As suggested by the videos, we developed it to the extent of testing the motor configuration and running the geometry of the plane through computer simulations in Abaqus and Star-CCM. With more time, we would have made further mechanical design adjustments and flight tuning.

This was definitely a huge source of inspiration for studying computer science post graduation. The code written for the Pixhawk flight controller was open source and available on Github. Most of the software was written in C++ from an object-oriented design approach.

  • Designed by: Joshua Ramayrat
  • Date: Fall 2017 - Winter 2017
  • Associates: Nick Gagliardi, Kiavash Moazzami, Francesca Caruso, Nicholas Keyes

Hole Punch

A simple hibiscus brings an otherwise dull product to life. This was made in my machining class where students were required to operate computerized milles, lathes, bandsaws, grinders, lasercutters, and more in our University's Machine Lab. Much geometric dimensioning & tolerancing was performed.

In the same breadth, there was a Junior Design competition as part of a machining class where students were asked to form teams and use the Machine Lab to build a low-cost chair for theoretical field use in 3rd world countries. This should've taken the duration of 5-10 weeks to complete. Our team took 1 week; we didn't use any of the equipment in the Machine Lab - just garage tools that we owned and basic pvc piping from home depot; and took 3rd place in the overall competition. An engineer shouldn't have to reinvent the wheel. (That's me in the background doing a child's pose!)

  • Designed by: Joshua Ramayrat
  • Date: Fall 2016 - Winter 2016
  • Associates: (chair) Westley Tusa, Christian Ruiz, Joseph Sahyoun, & Ruben Contreras.

Robot Arm

The goal of this project was to create a robotic arm with multiple degrees of freedom and control an end-effector (a syringe). It was completed at the end of my Junior Year in college after going through several iterations.

I'll be using this space to show documentation and steps that I took both in design, hardware, and software.


It was simple to attach joysticks to input pins of the arduino and call it. I wasn't very satisfied as this wasn't challenging at all. I've since moved onto the Beaglebone and I intend to learn how to program stepper motors using single-board computers once I've become more competent with C++.



  • Designed by: Joshua Ramayrat
  • Date: Summer 2015 - present
  • Keywords: Beagleboard, Linux, Stepper Motors, Mechanical Design
